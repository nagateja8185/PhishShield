Phishing Awareness Center

Learn to recognize and protect yourself from phishing attacks

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cybercrime in which attackers impersonate legitimate organizations or individuals to steal sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data. The term "phishing" is a play on the word "fishing" - attackers cast out bait (fraudulent messages) hoping to "catch" victims.

Key Statistics

  • Phishing accounts for over 80% of reported security incidents
  • A new phishing site is created every 20 seconds
  • 90% of data breaches involve phishing attacks
  • The average cost of a phishing attack for businesses is $4.65 million

Common Types of Phishing

📧 Email Phishing

The most common form. Attackers send fraudulent emails that appear to come from legitimate companies, asking recipients to click malicious links or provide sensitive information.

Example: An email claiming to be from your bank asking you to "verify your account" by clicking a link.

📱 Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or organizations. Attackers research their victims to create personalized and convincing messages.

Example: An email to a company's CFO appearing to come from the CEO requesting an urgent wire transfer.

💬 Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attacks conducted through text messages. These often contain urgent messages with malicious links.

Example: A text message claiming your package delivery failed, with a link to "reschedule."

📺 Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone-based phishing where attackers call victims pretending to be from legitimate organizations like banks or tech support.

Example: A call from someone claiming to be from "Microsoft Support" saying your computer has a virus.

🏠 Clone Phishing

Attackers create nearly identical copies (clones) of legitimate emails previously sent by the victim's contacts, but with malicious links or attachments.

🔗 Pharming

Redirecting website traffic to a fake website, even when the user types the correct URL. Often involves DNS poisoning or malware.

Warning Signs to Watch For

⚠

Urgent or Threatening Language

Messages creating a sense of urgency like "Your account will be suspended" or "Immediate action required."

🔑

Requests for Sensitive Information

Legitimate organizations never ask for passwords, PINs, or full credit card numbers via email.

🔗

Suspicious Links

Hover over links to see the actual URL. Look for misspelled domains or unusual characters.

👤

Generic Greetings

"Dear Customer" instead of your name. Legitimate companies usually personalize communications.

💬

Poor Grammar and Spelling

Many phishing emails contain obvious spelling mistakes and grammatical errors.

💰

Too-Good-To-Be-True Offers

Promises of prizes, lottery winnings, or inheritances from unknown relatives.

How to Protect Yourself

1

Think Before You Click

Hover over links to see the actual URL. Don't click on suspicious links in emails or messages.

2

Verify Sender Identity

Check the sender's email address carefully. When in doubt, contact the organization directly through official channels.

3

Use Multi-Factor Authentication

Enable MFA on all your accounts. Even if your password is stolen, attackers can't access your account without the second factor.

4

Keep Software Updated

Regularly update your operating system, browser, and antivirus software to protect against known vulnerabilities.

5

Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Use a password manager to create and store strong, unique passwords for each account.

6

Report Phishing Attempts

Report phishing emails to your IT department, email provider, or organizations being impersonated.
